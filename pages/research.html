// What We Investigate

Our Research

We develop the mathematical, computational, and observational tools needed to bring persistent awareness to the cislunar domain — the vast region between Earth and the Moon.

// Thrust 01

Cislunar Space Domain Awareness

As lunar exploration and commercial cislunar activity accelerate, the Earth-Moon system is becoming increasingly congested. Unlike the well-catalogued near-Earth regime, cislunar space presents fundamentally different dynamical and observational challenges: complex multi-body gravity, widely distributed trajectories, and sparse existing sensor coverage.

We leverage the geometry of the Bicircular Restricted Four-Body Problem (BCR4BP) to characterize natural optical observation regions in cislunar space — understanding where objects are visible, when, and from which vantage points. This foundational work underpins our observer design and trajectory optimization efforts.

// Thrust 02

Observer Architecture & Surveillance Trajectory Design

Knowing where to look is only part of the challenge — we must also decide where to put our observers. We formulate and solve optimization problems for placing ground-based stations (on Earth and the Moon) and designing space-based observer trajectories that maximize coverage of cislunar space.

Our ERAU FIRST grant (2026–2027) directly funds this thrust, developing optimized space-based observer architectures for sustained cislunar monitoring. We also study constellation design — how multiple observers can be coordinated for maximum collective coverage.

// Thrust 03

Multi-Sensor Fusion for Cislunar SSA

No single sensor type is sufficient for comprehensive cislunar surveillance. Optical telescopes excel at angular precision in dark conditions; radar systems provide range and velocity information but face range limitations at lunar distances. We develop frameworks to fuse these complementary data streams for enhanced detection and tracking.

Current work focuses on combining radar and optical observations to track resident space objects throughout the cislunar regime, including in the challenging near-lunar environment where Earth-based radar coverage degrades significantly.
